104th Birth Anniversary of Journalism Legend Zahur Hossain Chowdhury: The Life Story of a Fearless Pen-Warrior

Legacy Archive Desk | GenZ Frontier | June 27, 2026

Today, June 27, marks the 104th birth anniversary of Zahur Hossain Chowdhury—a prominent journalist, editor, columnist, and a pioneering figure of non-communal politics. He was born on this day in 1922.

In the journalism landscape of East Pakistan during the 1950s and 60s, a few rare luminaries turned their pens into formidable weapons for the exploited and working-class people; Zahur Hossain Chowdhury was one of the foremost among them. The trio of Zahur Hossain Chowdhury, Tofazzal Hossain Manik Miah, and Abdus Salam created a golden chapter in the history of East Bengal's journalism—an era that is still recognized as the foundational pillar of independent Bangladesh's media.

On the birthday of this pioneer and legendary figure of the newspaper world, the GenZ Frontier family pays deep and humble tribute to his enduring memory.

Birth and Educational Background

Zahur Hossain Chowdhury was born into a respectable family on June 27, 1922, in Ramnagar village under Daganbhuiyan upazila of the present-day Feni district. Displaying immense intellect from a young age, he earned his bachelor's degree with honors in History in 1942 from Presidency College, Kolkata—one of the most prestigious educational institutions in undivided Bengal. His progressive ideology and profound knowledge of history deeply influenced his journalistic and political perspectives in later life.

Foray into Journalism and the Golden Era of Editing

He dedicated the lion's share of his life to the newspaper and writing industry. His journalism career began with the famous magazine 'Bulbul', edited by Habibullah Bahar. He subsequently honed his professional skills by working for prominent English dailies published from Kolkata, including 'The Statesman', 'Comrade', and 'Star of India'.

Following the partition of the subcontinent in 1947, he migrated to East Bengal, where his struggle for independent journalism continued. During this period, he briefly worked for 'Upatt' and 'Pakistan Observer'.

The most significant chapter of his life began in 1951 when he joined the progressive daily newspaper 'Sangbad' as an assistant editor. Later, in 1954, he was appointed as the editor of the publication. He fulfilled his editorial duties with immense courage and competence for 17 long years, right up until the newspaper's office was burned down by the Pakistani occupation forces on the dark night of March 25, 1971, temporarily halting its publication.

After the country's independence, he edited an English periodical named 'Counter Point' and remained a lifelong director of 'Dainik Sangbad'.

The Immensely Popular Column 'Darbar-e-Zahur'

The sharpest and most popular aspect of Zahur Hossain Chowdhury's pen was reflected in his columns. The regular column he wrote under the title 'Darbar-e-Zahur' in the pages of Dainik Sangbad was massively popular among politically conscious readers of that era.

Through this column, he mobilized public opinion against the political conspiracies, economic exploitation, communalism, and anti-humanitarian activities of the West Pakistani ruling elites, using fluent yet piercing language. A selected compilation of these columns was published as a book titled 'Darbar-e-Zahur' in 1985, which remains an invaluable document of Bangladesh's political history.

Political Ideology and Contributions as an Organizer

Beyond journalism, his role as a political and social organizer was unforgettable. In his youth, he was associated with the Muslim Chhatra League and M.N. Roy's Radical Democratic Party. Later in the 1950s, he was elected as a member of the provincial committee of the progressive political party, National Awami Party (NAP).

He was always vocal in demanding the rights of journalists. He was one of the key architects behind the establishment of the East Pakistan Union of Journalists (EPUJ) and the East Pakistan Press Club (now the National Press Club). Furthermore, aiming to improve international cultural and diplomatic relations, he served as the founding secretary of the then Pak-China Friendship Society and the Pak-Soviet Friendship Society.

Honors and Demise

This fearless pen-warrior and intellectual breathed his last on December 11, 1980. In recognition of his outstanding contributions to journalism, the Government of Bangladesh posthumously awarded him the state's highest civilian honor, the 'Ekushey Padak', in 1981. To keep his ideals and memory alive, 'Dainik Sangbad' later introduced the 'Zahur Hossain Memorial Award', a prestigious accolade in the country's journalism sector.

Zahur Hossain Chowdhury's life teaches us how a sharpened pen can become mightier than bullets and autocracy. His ideals and objectivity will forever remain exemplary for the new generation of journalists.

2. Which newspaper is he most famous for editing? He is most widely known for serving as the editor of the progressive daily newspaper 'Sangbad' for a long tenure (1954–1971).

3. What was the name of his famous column? His highly popular column, published in Dainik Sangbad, was named 'Darbar-e-Zahur'.

4. Which journalistic organizations did he help establish? He played a leading role in establishing the East Pakistan Union of Journalists (EPUJ) and the East Pakistan Press Club (currently the National Press Club).

5. What state honor did he receive? For his extraordinary contribution to journalism, the Government of Bangladesh posthumously awarded him the 'Ekushey Padak' in 1981.

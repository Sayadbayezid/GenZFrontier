Opta Reveals World Cup 2026 Group Stage Best XI: Who Joined Superstars Messi and Mbappé?

Sports Analytics Desk | GenZ Frontier | June 29, 2026

With an action-packed 72 matches of the FIFA World Cup 2026 group stage officially wrapped up and the high-stakes Round of 32 already underway across North America, the tournament has provided a perfect blend of historic individual brilliance, tactical masterclasses, and shocking underdog stories.

To map out who truly performed at an elite level during the opening phase, the renowned sports data analytics platform Opta Analyst has unveiled its comprehensive Group Stage Best XI. Using advanced performance metrics, tracking data, and underlying efficiency ratings, the analytics giant has built a balanced, powerhouse lineup. While global icons like Lionel Messi and Kylian Mbappé spearhead the squad, they are joined by a collection of defensive stalwarts and midfield engines who completely defied expectations on football's grandest stage.

Defensive Line: The Impenetrable Back Five

The group stage proved that solid defensive structures are the bedrock of tournament survival. Opta's selection rewards individual tenacity, clinical ball-distribution from the back, and elite shot-stopping.

Goalkeeper: Vozinha (Cape Verde)

Age is just a number for Cape Verde’s 40-year-old shot-stopper, Vozinha, who stood out as an absolute wall in the opening round. He single-handedly kept his team alive in their monumental opener against Spain. Despite conceding twice against a relentless Uruguay attack, he bounced back to cap off the group stage with a flawless, heroic display against Saudi Arabia. Statistically, no goalkeeper in the entire tournament recorded more clean sheets than Vozinha during the group stage, drawing level only with Spain’s elite number one, Unai Simón.

Right-Back: Marvin Senaya (Ghana)

Ghana's dynamic fullback Marvin Senaya produced what many tactical analysts consider the single finest defensive fullback masterclass of the tournament during his side's encounter against England. Senaya was a relentless force on the right flank, leading the entire group stage with a staggering 18 successful tackles. He completely dominated his individual battles, winning 24 out of 38 duels. His precision in one-on-one containment was further backed by a 72 percent ‘True Tackles’ success rate—a high-level Opta metric that specifically rewards a defender for executing deliberate contact with the ball even when the opposing attacker temporarily retains possession.

Centre-Back: Diney Borges (Cape Verde)

Complementing his goalkeeper’s heroic efforts, Cape Verdean centre-back Diney Borges emerged as a towering pillar of stability. Borges read the game beautifully, ranking second across the entire tournament for total clearances with 31 critical interventions. Among all tournament defenders who engaged in at least 20 active duels, Borges boasted a superior aerial and ground success rate of 68 percent. Furthermore, his aggressive defensive instincts allowed him to record eight completed tackles during the opening round, a feat surpassed by only two other centre-backs in the competition.

Centre-Back: Pau Cubarsí (Spain)

At just 19 years old, Spain’s teenage sensation Pau Cubarsí displayed a mature, instinctive positional awareness that completely belies his lack of international experience. With 16 ‘possession regains’ (stripping the ball cleanly away from an opponent's feet), he ranks comfortably as the fourth-best defensive centre-back in the tournament. However, it is Cubarsí’s breathtaking composure on the ball that truly sets him apart. Out of his 294 attempted passes in the group stage, only five failed to find their target. He also ranks fifth overall for ‘line-breaking passes,’ successfully slicing through opposition midfields 39 times to trigger Spanish attacks.

Left-Back: Keito Nakamura (Japan)

Operating as a hyper-aggressive, inverted option on Japan’s left flank, Nakamura utilized his blistering acceleration and low center of gravity to devastating effect. Far from a traditional defensive left-back, he acted as the primary offensive architect behind the Samurai Blue’s opening goals against both the Netherlands and Tunisia. Nakamura was incredibly efficient in transition, completing five out of his seven attempted high-speed dribbles and involving himself directly in nine distinct attacking sequences—the fifth-highest metric recorded among all fullbacks in the tournament.

Midfield Engine Room: Power, Aggression, and Spatial Control

The central midfield pairing in Opta's Best XI features a perfect combination of raw physical drive and relentless defensive work-rate mixed with elite playmaking vision.

Central Midfield: Weston McKennie (USA)

Deployed in a far more advanced, aggressive box-to-box role by the USMNT coaching staff, Weston McKennie provided the clinical, sharp edge to the United States' transitional attack. His bull-like physical strength and ability to drive vertically through the center of the pitch made his forward ball-carrying almost impossible for opposing midfielders to halt. By creating seven clear-cut goal-scoring chances from open play, McKennie firmly established his status within the top tier of international midfielders throughout the opening phase.

Central Midfield: Pedro Vite (Ecuador)

Ecuador’s rising star Pedro Vite proved to be an absolute, indestructible engine in the heart of the midfield. Vite was the complete package: remarkably comfortable under heavy pressure, creative in his distribution, and absolutely relentless when tracking back to win the ball. Only four players in the entire tournament created more chances from open play than Vite (8), and he ranked third overall in total tackles made (14). In terms of sheer ball-recovery metrics, only European stalwarts Granit Xhaka (24) and Rodrigo Bentancur (29) managed to regain possession more times than Vite (23), who also showcased his passing efficiency by completing 234 passes.

The Attacking Vanguard: Global Megastars and Clinical Finishers

The front line of Opta’s Best XI is a frighteningly lethal combination of footballing royalty and next-generation clinical goalscorers, accumulating a combined 20 goals in the group stages alone.

Right Wing: Lionel Messi (Argentina)

Lionel Messi continues to redefine the boundaries of footballing longevity, standing out as the tournament’s absolute blueprint of excellence. The Argentine captain made history yet again by becoming the first player ever to score in seven consecutive World Cup matches across multiple editions. In this current 2026 iteration alone, Messi has already weaponized La Albiceleste’s attack, netting a phenomenal six goals in just three group-stage matches. As Argentina prepares for a high-intensity Round of 32 clash against Cape Verde, the statistical models indicate his clinical goal-scoring form shows no signs of slowing down.

Left Wing: Vinícius Júnior (Brazil)

Vinícius Júnior has officially unlocked his most terrifying, unguardable form in the iconic yellow Seleção jersey, terrorizing right-backs and scoring in all three consecutive group-stage matches. For Brazil, his consistent output represents far more than just individual statistics; it acts as a historic lucky charm. In the three previous World Cup tournaments where Brazil managed to score in every single group stage match, they went on to lift the golden trophy. Vinícius' combination of high-speed dribbling and vastly improved finishing positioning makes him the most dangerous winger in the tournament.

Forward: Erling Haaland (Norway)

The towering Norwegian striker has been a clinical monster in front of goal, finding the back of the net four times in just two matches. Only Lionel Messi boasts a higher goal tally. What makes Haaland’s inclusion so staggering is the underlying efficiency; even without the inflation of penalty kicks, his non-penalty Expected Goals (npxG) rating stands at a phenomenal 2.68—the second-highest in the entire tournament, trailing only Vinícius Júnior. Among all tournament forwards who registered at least five shots on target, Haaland ranks fifth in the world with a deadly 40 percent shot-conversion rate.

Forward: Kylian Mbappé (France)

Kylian Mbappé continues to prove that the World Cup stage is his personal playground. With his breathtaking displays in the group phase, the French captain became only the fourth player in the long history of the sport to score two or more goals in three consecutive World Cup matches. Having already accumulated a jaw-dropping 16 career World Cup goals at just 27 years old, Mbappé is now breathing directly down the neck of Lionel Messi and Miroslav Klose for the all-time tournament record. Whether he is blasting the ball into the top corner himself or dropping deep to playmake—as seen during his brilliant assist for Ousmane Dembélé’s spectacular brace against Norway—Mbappé remains the ultimate big-game player.

Cape Verde was the most prominent underdog nation to secure multiple spots in the elite lineup, with 40-year-old veteran goalkeeper Vozinha and star centre-back Diney Borges both making the squad due to their defensive records.

3. What historic goal-scoring records were highlighted in the group stage? 4. Why was Spain's Pau Cubarsí included over more experienced defenders?
